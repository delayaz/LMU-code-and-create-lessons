The 4 project tracks

Every track uses the same toolbox you already have. Pick whichever sounds most fun to you — not whichever sounds "easiest." You'll have more energy for a project you actually want to build.

Track 1

Game

A number-guessing game leveled up, a text-adventure, rock-paper-scissors tournament, or a dice/card game.

Uses: the random module, a game loop that keeps playing until someone wins or loses.

Track 2

Quiz App

A personality quiz, a trivia quiz with scoring, or a study tool for a class you're taking.

Uses: questions stored as a list of dictionaries, tallying score across a loop.

Track 3

Interactive Story

A choose-your-own-adventure with branching paths, or a "build your character" story generator.

Uses: the conditionals/branching logic from Day 2, organized cleanly with functions.

Track 4

Data-Analysis Tool

Analyze a small dataset — grades, sports stats, screen-time logs — and report simple stats.

Uses: lists/dictionaries from Day 5, plus sum(), max(), min(), averages.

!None of these tracks are locked in stone — a "game" with a quiz-like scoring system, or a "quiz" that's secretly about your own data, are both totally fine. The track is a starting point, not a cage.

Get your starter file set up

Before you sketch your idea on paper, get the actual skeleton file into your repl. This is the same structure for every track — you'll fill in the specifics once you know what you're building.

main.py — starter template
# SECTION 1: YOUR PROJECT'S DATA
# Whatever your project needs to remember goes here.
project_title = "My Project"
sample_data = [
    {"name": "Example One", "value": 10},
    {"name": "Example Two", "value": 25},
]

# SECTION 2: YOUR PROJECT'S FUNCTIONS
def show_welcome():
    print(f"=== {project_title} ===")

def show_data():
    for item in sample_data:
        print(f"{item['name']}: {item['value']}")

# SECTION 3: MAIN PROGRAM
show_welcome()
show_data()
Your turn

Copy this into a fresh repl, run it, then make it yours

Get this exact starter running first — confirm it prints without errors. Then start replacing the placeholder data and function names with whatever fits your actual project idea.

  • Rename project_title to your real project's name
  • Replace sample_data with a rough sketch of what your project needs to track
  • Don't worry about getting it "right" yet — this is scaffolding, not a final answer
!Every one of today's 4 tracks works completely fine built this way — text in, text out, no special setup needed. That's a real, complete way to ship a project, not a placeholder for something fancier.

Part 310:30 – 11:20

From "I have to pick a track" to "I know what I'm building"

Going straight from a track name to a finished idea is hard. These four steps narrow things down gradually — each one builds on the last, so by the end you're not guessing, you're refining something you've already started.

Step 1 — 5 min

Brainstorm wide — write down 5 ideas, don't judge them yet

Within your chosen track, write 5 different specific ideas as fast as you can. Quantity over quality here — even bad or boring ideas are useful, because writing them down clears space for better ones.

  • Game track: "5 different games I could build" — not just "a game"
  • Quiz track: "5 different quiz topics" — what would you actually want to take?
  • Story track: "5 different settings or situations" — a haunted house, a locker room, a spaceship...
  • Data track: "5 different things I could collect data about" — your own habits, sports stats, song lengths...
Step 2 — 5 min

Narrow to one — pick using two filters

Look at your 5 ideas. Cross off any that fail either of these two tests:

  • Can I picture the whole thing? If you can't imagine someone actually using it start to finish, it's too vague right now — cross it off or set it aside.
  • Do I actually want to spend 3 days on this? Not "is it impressive" — just, would building this hold your attention. That matters more than ambition here.

Whatever survives both filters — even if it's not your "best" idea on paper — is your project. Circle it.

Step 3 — 10 min

Flesh it out — the 3-box sketch

Now go deep on the one idea you circled. Answer all three:

  • What does it do? One or two sentences. What's the actual point of your project?
  • What does the user see first? The very first thing printed when it starts.
  • What happens when they interact with it? Walk through one full example — a guess, an answer, a choice — start to finish, like you're narrating someone using it.
Step 4 — 10 min

Pressure-test it — pair up and pitch out loud

Find a partner (doesn't need to be the same track) and explain your 3-box sketch to them in under 2 minutes, like you're describing it to someone who's never seen it. Then they ask you one question — anything that's unclear or makes them curious.

  • If their question stumps you, that's not a failure — that's exactly the gap in your plan worth fixing before you start coding, not after.
  • Switch roles, do the same for their idea.
!If you're between two ideas after Step 2, just pick one — you can always build the other later. A specific, finished small project beats a vague, ambitious one every time in a course this short.
